Internal research told me how the machine worked from the inside. It did not tell me how the machine felt from the outside, to the sellers whose listings got touched by it and the customers who filed the reports that started a case in the first place. That is a different conversation, and it needed a different amount of care to arrange.

## Getting to this research took longer than I expected

Talking to reviewers was mostly a scheduling problem. Talking to actual sellers and customers involved legal and privacy review first, since anything I asked could not accidentally reveal how the detection or policy logic worked, and could not create any impression that the outcome of a specific case was negotiable. That review process took real time, and I will admit I underestimated it in my early planning. A good chunk of what I thought would be a two week research push turned into closer to five weeks once legal review was factored in.

Once cleared, I worked with our research team to recruit a mix of sellers who had gone through the appeals process recently, and customers who had filed a report that led to a case. Both groups agreed to talk on the record about their experience, without getting into the specifics of any single decision. We also agreed on a short, fixed list of questions ahead of time with legal, so I was not improvising in the moment and accidentally asking something I should not have.

I want to be fair to legal here, since it would be easy to write this as a story about bureaucracy slowing things down. The concerns were reasonable. A seller research session is not the place to accidentally signal how detection logic works, and a customer research session is not the place to imply an outcome might be reversed as a courtesy for participating. Once I understood the reasoning, the extra weeks felt less like friction and more like a cost worth paying, even though it was a genuine cost to the project timeline.

## What sellers actually said

The dominant theme from sellers was not disagreement with outcomes, which honestly surprised me a little. Most sellers, even ones whose listing had been removed, understood on some level that the rules existed for a reason. What frustrated them was not knowing why a specific decision had been made, and not having a clear, fast way to ask.

One seller described getting a notice that her listing had been removed, with language general enough that she could not tell which part of her listing had triggered it. She resubmitted a nearly identical listing, guessing at what might have been the issue, and it got removed again for the same reason she still did not fully understand. That is a genuinely bad experience, and it was not caused by the underlying policy being wrong. It was caused by the explanation reaching her being too thin to actually act on.

Sellers also brought up how long appeals took, and how opaque the appeal process felt while it was in progress. A few described it as sending a message into a box and waiting, with no visibility into whether a person was even looking at it yet. One seller who ran a small business full time told me the uncertainty was almost worse than a firm no. A firm no, even one she disagreed with, she could plan around. A message sitting in a queue with no timeline meant she genuinely did not know whether to keep waiting or start finding another way to sell the same item elsewhere.

A smaller theme, but one that came up more than once, was language. A couple of sellers were not native English speakers, and the notices they received were dense enough in plain English, let alone translated. One mentioned running the notice through a translation tool herself just to be sure she understood which policy had been cited.

## What customers actually said

Customers who had filed a report cared about a different thing almost entirely. They mostly were not trying to track the outcome of their specific report. What they wanted was confidence that reporting something concerning actually did something, that it was not disappearing into a void. A few customers told me they had stopped bothering to report things because they had reported similar issues before and never heard anything back, and had no way to know if action was taken.

That is a trust problem more than a process problem, and it sat slightly outside the scope of what I was originally asked to fix. I flagged it anyway, because a workflow that gets faster and more consistent on the inside but still leaves the person who raised the original concern in the dark has only solved half the actual problem.

One customer put it in a way I kept coming back to later. She said she did not need to know the outcome, she just wanted to know someone had actually looked. That is a low bar, and the fact that the existing process could not reliably clear even that low bar told me something about how much of this problem was communication rather than judgment.

## Where this left me

Between internal and external research, I now had two lists that did not fully overlap. Internally, the ask was consistency and better routing of hard cases. Externally, the ask was clearer communication and visible follow through. Neither side was wrong, they were just looking at different parts of the same system. Making sense of both lists together, and turning them into something I could actually design against, is where the next post starts.
