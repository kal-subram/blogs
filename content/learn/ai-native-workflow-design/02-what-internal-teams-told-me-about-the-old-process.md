Once I understood the shape of the old process, my instinct as a PM was to start sketching a solution. I have learned to distrust that instinct now, but back then it took real effort to sit on it. Before I sketched anything, I needed to talk to the people already living inside this process, not the dashboards about them.

## Who I actually talked to

Three groups mattered here, and each one saw the process differently.

Reviewers were the obvious starting point, since they touched every case directly. I spent time shadowing a handful of them, watching how they actually worked rather than asking them to describe it from memory. Memory is a generous editor. Watching someone work a real queue for an hour told me things a survey never would have.

Team leads were the second group, and they saw a different layer of the same problem. They cared about consistency across their reviewers, about training new hires fast enough to keep up with attrition, and about the awkward conversations that came up when two reviewers on their team had handled near identical cases differently.

Policy owners were the third group, and honestly the one I underestimated going in. They were the people who wrote the guidance reviewers were supposed to follow, and they had their own frustration, watching policy get applied inconsistently no matter how carefully they wrote it. A few of them had tried rewriting sections of the guidance more than once, hoping clearer language would fix the consistency problem. It helped a little, but not nearly as much as they hoped.

## Why I added a survey on top of interviews

Shadowing and interviews are slow by nature. I could realistically sit with a dozen or so reviewers across a few weeks, which is enough to find real signal but not enough to know how widely that signal held across the whole team, which was spread across more than one site.

So I put together a short internal survey, maybe fifteen questions, sent to every reviewer on the team, not just the ones I had already talked to. Nothing fancy, mostly questions about which parts of their day felt like wasted effort, which cases they dreaded seeing, and how confident they felt in their own decisions on the harder ones. The response rate was good, reviewers clearly wanted to be heard on this, and the results largely confirmed what shadowing had already suggested, which was reassuring rather than redundant. When your qualitative and quantitative research agree with each other, that is usually a sign you are looking at something real rather than a story from one particularly vocal reviewer.

## What shadowing sessions actually surfaced

Sitting with reviewers directly surfaced things that never showed up in any report I had read before this. One reviewer told me, almost in passing, that a good chunk of her queue felt obviously fine within the first ten seconds of opening a case, but the process still required her to click through every field and leave a note before closing it. That clicking added minutes to cases that did not need minutes.

Another reviewer, more experienced, said the opposite problem bothered her more. The genuinely hard cases, the ones with real ambiguity, arrived in the same queue as the easy ones, with no signal upfront about which was which. She had to open a case fully before she could tell whether it deserved five minutes or forty five.

A few reviewers also brought up something I had not expected at all, which was quiet anxiety about where this project was headed. A couple asked directly whether I was there to replace their jobs. I was honest that I did not fully know yet, and that the goal was to remove the parts of their job that were not actually using their judgment, not to remove them. I do not think that fully settled anyone's nerves, and I do not think it should have on day one. Trust like that gets earned later, through what actually gets shipped, not through what a PM says in a research session.

## What team leads and policy owners added

Team leads gave me a clearer picture of the training problem. A new reviewer took months to get genuinely reliable on the harder edge cases, and in that ramp period, quality was noticeably lower. With attrition running the way it usually does on a team like this, a meaningful share of the team was always somewhere in that ramp period, which quietly dragged down consistency across the board.

Policy owners gave me the most useful reframe of the whole round of research. One of them said something like, the guidance is not actually the problem, the guidance is trying to describe judgment in words, and judgment does not compress into words as cleanly as we would like. That stuck with me. It meant the fix probably was not going to be better written policy. It was going to be something that could apply judgment more consistently than a tired reviewer on hour six of a shift, while still knowing when to hand a case to a person.

## What I got wrong going in

I want to be honest about one assumption I walked in with, because it was wrong. I assumed the biggest opportunity was simply speed, getting cases through the queue faster. What actually came out of these conversations was that consistency and correctly routing the hard cases to the right attention mattered more to almost everyone I spoke with than raw speed. Reviewers wanted less noise in their queue, not just a faster queue. Team leads wanted predictable quality, not just predictable throughput. That reframing changed what I went looking for once I started talking to customers, which is where the next post picks up.
