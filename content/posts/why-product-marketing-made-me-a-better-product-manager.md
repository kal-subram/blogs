For a long time, I thought product marketing was the part that came after the real product work was done.

Engineering built the thing. Product decided the roadmap. PMM made it sound good.

Very clean division of labor. Also very wrong.

The more time I spent around product marketing, the more I understood that strong PMM is not decorative. It is not just launch slides, naming, messaging, or the polite clean-up crew that arrives when everyone is tired and the product needs a story by Friday.

Good product marketing sits much closer to product truth. It forces a team to face whether the thing they built can actually be understood by the people who have to sell it, support it, and trust it, and not just by the people who built it.

That changed me as a product manager.

My career has moved through engineering, reliability, enterprise analytics, product marketing, and AI systems. At the time these felt like separate chapters. Looking back, they were really the same question asked in different rooms: does the system work, does it fail gracefully, and does anyone outside the room understand why it matters.

Comcast was one of the places where this became very clear.

I came from the engineering side, where the world was concrete. APIs worked or they did not. Pipelines passed data or they failed. Capacity was enough or it was not. If TPS was low, nobody cared that the slide looked beautiful. The system complained, and production systems do not complain softly.

That background gave me a deep respect for reliability. Product marketing needed a different discipline altogether. Even when a product works technically, it still has to land with people, who need to understand what changed, why it matters, and why they should believe it.

That part is easy to underrate.

## Making speed visible

One public Comcast example I like is the California Drone Speed Challenge, hosted with Aerial Sports League at the Palace of Fine Arts in San Francisco. Comcast used the event to promote Xfinity Gigabit Internet speeds by connecting fast internet to drone racing, a sport built around speed, reflexes, latency, and spectacle. The event brought together some of California's fastest pilots and tied the experience to STEM and community outreach through a donation to Girls in Tech.

The interesting part was not simply that the event was flashy. Of course it was flashy. Put neon lights, drones, and fast pilots in a room and people will look up from their phones. We are all human only.

That's really the PMM move here: turning an invisible benefit into something visible.

Internet speed can become abstract very quickly. Gigabit speeds, latency, throughput, bandwidth, connected homes, business connectivity, and all that: these are technically meaningful, but not always something people feel. Customers can understand the words and still not feel the difference.

Drone racing changes that. Speed becomes something you can see. You can hear it. You can feel the tension in the room. The product benefit stops being only a claim and becomes an experience.

## The last mile is not just sales

Ask an engineering team where the last mile is and they'll say distribution. Ask a product team and they'll say launch. Neither is quite it. The last mile is understanding.

This matters even more in enterprise and technical products. A team can spend months building something useful, then assume adoption will happen because the feature is obviously valuable. But what's obvious to the builder is rarely obvious to the customer.

The builder has lived inside the product for months, sometimes years. The customer hasn't. Neither has the seller, the support team, or the executive sponsor who has to justify the renewal. Everyone shows up with different context, different incentives, and a different tolerance for confusion.

A product can be technically correct and still fail to land. I've watched it happen more than once: the feature works, the demo is thorough, and the buyer still doesn't see themselves in it. Then everyone calls it a slow-adoption problem, as if adoption were just weather that happened to us.

Product marketing made me less tolerant of that kind of vagueness, and quicker to notice when a product was leaning too hard on explanation. If every customer conversation needed heroic storytelling to land, the product or the positioning needed work.

It forced the clarity earlier, while the decisions were still being made, instead of after launch when field teams were already improvising.

## A demo is not a feature parade

One thing I learned doing outbound product work is that a demo should never be a walking tour through every capability.

Nobody wants to be taken through the entire feature garden. See this button, see this filter, see this dashboard, see this export. By minute seven, people are smiling politely and making grocery lists in their head.

![A large crowd seen from behind, in black and white, rows of heads and shoulders stretching into the distance](/images/posts/why-product-marketing-made-me-a-better-product-manager-demo.jpg)
*This is what minute seven of a feature-tour demo looks like from the stage.*

A good demo has movement. It starts from a problem the audience already recognizes, shows what's painful about it today, and only then brings the product in, not as a hero riding in with background music, just as a better way to work.

This was especially true in enterprise analytics and technical products. Customers didn't want a generic capability tour. They wanted to know the product understood their business, and they wanted to feel it, not just hear it: "Yes, this is my problem."

This carried straight into my later AI work. AI products are especially vulnerable to bad demos, because the capability can look magical for five minutes, and magic is not what gets something adopted. Adoption comes from trust, usefulness, and fit.

## PMM made me more allergic to vague AI language

When I moved deeper into AI systems, my product marketing background became surprisingly useful.

AI has a language problem. The industry loves broad capability words: automate, summarize, generate, classify, reason, assist, transform. These words aren't wrong, but they're often too loose. They create excitement without enough operational meaning.

In real workflows, people need more clarity: what the system will do, what it won't, when it defers, how errors get handled, and what judgment a person still has in the loop. That's part of responsible product design, not just messaging.

A PMM mindset cuts through vague promises by forcing specifics: automation for which workflow, under what conditions, with what a person can actually inspect, appeal, or override when the system gets it wrong, rather than a comforting label like "AI-powered automation."

I like my PPTs, fully guilty. But I have less patience now for AI language that makes everyone nod for 30 minutes and then quietly panic later.

The real work is just making complicated systems understandable, without pretending they're simpler than they are.

## PMM is strategy in public

The biggest misconception about product marketing is that it comes after strategy.

In strong teams, PMM is strategy made public.

PMM exposes where the story is weak: where customer understanding breaks, where field teams are improvising because the positioning isn't clear, where the promise is too broad, too narrow, or just not believable.

For a product manager, that feedback is gold. It shows where the product hasn't fully landed yet.

This changed how I think about adoption. It's not really a launch problem, it's a product design problem: a product that can't be explained clearly is rarely as ready as the team thinks, and if sellers can't position it, it'll struggle, and if users misread it, that shows up later as support tickets, mistrust, or quiet non-use.

Some of this sounds obvious, but many teams learn it late. Usually after the launch deck is done, the announcement is out, and everyone is wondering why the market is not behaving according to plan. Very inconsiderate of the market, but what to do.

## What I carried forward

Engineering left me trusting systems. Reliability work left me trusting them a little less, in a useful way. And product marketing is where I finally understood that being right and being understood are not the same thing, not even close.

That combination shaped how I work as a product manager.

A product has to work, and it has to be reliable, but it also has to be understandable enough that people know why it matters and can trust it. That matters even more in AI, where the system isn't always deterministic and the user can't always see what's happening underneath.

It trained me to look beyond the build, to care about how a product is explained, adopted, trusted, remembered. It also made me more honest. If a product needs too much translation to land, something in it is probably unfinished.

The best products do not just function. They land.

They land because the product is real, the story is clear, and the promise survives contact with customers. That's the kind of product work I care about: building the thing, yes, but also making sure it's understood well enough to actually matter.
